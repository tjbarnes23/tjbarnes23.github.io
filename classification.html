4. Method Classification

A.
Definitions
1.
Method Class

A group of Methods that contain the same, defined features.

Further explanation: The following diagram shows how Methods are classified. The terms used in the diagram are all defined below. Terms shown in bold form a Method's Class Name (see Section E below).

2.
Hunt Bell

A bell that ends a Plain Lead in the same Place as it started.

Example:

Further explanation: The place notation recorded in the Central Council’s Method Library for the Method Plain Bob Minimus is x14x14x14x12. This place notation produces one Plain Lead when applied once to an initial Row. Using an initial Row of 1234 (Rounds), the Plain Lead shown in the diagram is produced. To identify which are the Hunt Bell(s), look at the final Row produced (in this example it is 1342). The Hunt Bell(s) are the bell(s) that are in the same Place(s) in the final Row as they were in the initial Row. In Plain Bob Minimus, the bell in 1st’s Place in the initial Row (in this example, the treble) is therefore the only Hunt Bell.

Technical comments: Note that a Plain Lead is defined in terms of a Static Method. The concept of a Hunt Bell may not apply to Dynamic Methods.

3.
Working Bell

A bell that ends a Plain Lead in a different Place from where it started.

Further explanation: Using the example in A.2 above, the bells in 2nd's, 3rd's and 4th's Places in the initial Row (in this example, the 2nd, 3rd and 4th respectively) are Working Bells because at the final Row of a Plain Lead they have not returned to the same Places they were in at the initial Row.

Technical comments: Note that a Plain Lead is defined in terms of a Static Method. The concept of a Working Bell may not apply to Dynamic Methods.

4.
Stationary Bell

A Hunt Bell that occupies the same Place in every Row of a Plain Lead.

Example: 3rd's Place bell in Martyrs Link Little Bob Major is a Stationary Bell.
(Note that this Method will be reclassified under this proposed framework and will no longer be a Little Bob Method.)

5.
Path

The sequence of Places that a given bell occupies as it progresses through the Rows of a Block.

Further explanation: A Path is often highlighted by drawing a line through the bell in question. The treble’s Path is shown this way using a red line in the example in A.2 above.

6.
Hunting

A Path that is formed by progressing from an earlier Place to a later Place, or vice versa (but not a combination of both), at the rate of one Place per Row. (Alternative form: a bell Hunts.)

Further explanation: In the diagram in A.2 above, the treble Hunts up in the first half of the lead, and then Hunts down in the second half of the lead.

7.
Place-Making

Occupying the same Place for two or more consecutive Rows.

Further explanation: See the example of Place-Making in C.1.1 below.

8.
Making a Place

Occupying the same Place for exactly two consecutive Rows. (Alternative form: a bell Makes a Place.)

Further explanation: In the example in A.2 above, the treble Makes 4th's at the half lead, and Makes 1st's at the lead end.

Note that while Making a Place refers to occupying the same Place for only two Rows, other forms also exist. For example, occupying the same Place for 3 Rows, which would be Place-Making under A.6 above, might also be referred to as 'making a place twice'.

9.
Making an Internal Place

Making a Place that is neither the first Place nor the last Place of the Block in question. (Alternative form: a bell Makes an Internal Place.)

Further explanation: See the examples of Making an Internal Place in C.2.2 and C.2.3 below.

10.
Dodging Places

Successive pairs of adjacent Places of the Path of the Hunt Bell in question.

Example: In a Path where the Hunt Bell moves between 1st's Place and 6th's Place, the Dodging Places are 1-2, 3-4 and 5-6.

Further explanation: In a Hunt Bell Path that is Little (see below), the pairs of Dodging Places are counted from the lowest Place through which the Hunt Bell passes. For example, if the Hunt Bell moves between 4th's Place and 9th's Place, the Dodging Places are 4-5, 6-7 and 8-9.
If the Hunt Bell moves through a Path that has an odd number of Places, the last Place is considered to be its own Dodging Place. For example, if the Hunt Bell moves between 3rd's Place and 7th's Place, the Dodging Places are 3-4, 5-6 and 7.

11.
Cross Section

A Change at which the Hunt Bell in question crosses from one pair of Dodging Places to the next.

Example: In a Path where the Hunt Bell moves between 1st's Place and 6th's Place, since the Dodging Places are 1-2, 3-4 and 5-6, the Cross Sections are the Changes that cause the Hunt Bell in question to:
(1) move from 2nd's Place to 3rd’s Place;
(2) move from 4th’s Place to 5th’s Place;
(3) move from 5th’s Place to 4th’s Place; and
(4) move from 3rd’s Place to 2nd’s Place.

Further explanation: See the examples in C.2.1, C.2.2 and C.2.3 below.

B.
Method Classes: General
1.
Jump Method

A Method that uses one or more Jump Changes.

2.
Hunter

A Static Method that has one or more Hunt Bells and which does not use Jump Changes.

Example:

Further explanation: A Plain Lead of the Method x14x14x14x12, starting from Rounds, ends with the Row 1342, as shown in the diagram. At the end of one Plain Lead, the treble is the only bell that has returned to the Place it was in at the initial Row (1234), so the treble is this Method's only Hunt Bell. Continuing to apply the place notation successively produces the 2 additional leads shown, such that after 3 Plain Leads, Rounds is obtained. This is the Method’s Plain Course. Since (1) this Method has at least one Hunt Bell and (2) all Working Bells (which in this case are bells 2, 3 and 4) first return to their starting Places after the same number of Plain Leads (in this case 3 Plain Leads), it is a Hunter. (This Method is Plain Bob Minimus.)

3.
Principle

A Static Method that has no Hunt Bells and which does not use Jump Changes.

Example:

Further explanation: A Plain Lead of the Method 34x34.14.12.14, starting from Rounds, ends with the Row 2341, as shown in the diagram. At the end of one Plain Lead, no bell has returned to the Place it was in at the initial Row (1234), so this method has no Hunt Bells. Continuing to apply the place notation successively produces the 3 additional leads shown, such that after 4 Plain Leads, Rounds is obtained. This is the Method’s Plain Course. Since (1) this Method has no Hunt Bells and (2) all Working Bells (which in this case is all bells because there are no Hunt Bells) first return to their starting Places after the same number of Plain Leads (in this case 4 Plain Leads), it is a Principle. (This Method is Stanton Minimus.)

4.
Differential Method

A Hunter or Principle in which the Working Bells do not all first return to their starting Places after the same number of Plain Leads as there are Working Bells.

Example:

Further explanation: A Plain Lead of the Method 5.1.5.1.5.1.5.1.5.345, starting from rounds, ends with the Row 31254, as shown in the diagram. At the end of one Plain Lead, no bell has returned to the Place it was in at the initial Row (12345), so this Method has no Hunt Bells. All bells are therefore Working Bells. Continuing to apply the place notation successively produces the 5 additional leads shown, such that after 6 Plain Leads, Rounds is obtained. This is the Method’s Plain Course. It will be seen that bells 4 and 5 return to their starting Places at the end of the 2nd, 4th and 6th leads, whereas bells 1, 2 and 3 return to their starting Places at the end of the 3rd and 6th leads. Because the Working Bells do not all first return to their starting Places after the same number of Plain Leads, this Method is a Differential. Because it also has no Hunt Bells, it is a Differential Principle. (This Method is Christ Church Dublin Differential Doubles.)

While the example above shows a Principle that is Differential, Hunters can also be Differential. See Deferential Differential Bob Minor for example.

C.
Method Classes: Hunters with One Hunt Bell
1.
Plain Method

A Hunter in which:
1. The Path of the Hunt Bell is the same if it is rung backwards;
2. The Hunt Bell rings exactly twice in each Place of the Path during a Plain Lead; and
3. The Hunt Bell is not a Stationary Bell.

Example:

Further explanation: In this Method, the treble (which is the only hunt bell) rings two times in each Place in a Plain Lead. The treble also has the same Path if it is rung forwards or backwards. This Method is therefore a Plain Method. (This Method is Plain Bob Minor.)

1.1.
Place Method

A Plain Method in which the Paths of all the bells consist only of Hunting and Place-Making, and in which a change in the direction of Hunting is separated by Place-Making.

Example:

Further explanation: In this Plain Method, changes in the direction of Hunting by all bells are always separated by the Making of at least one Place. This Method is therefore a Place Method. (This Method is Foti Place Minor.)

1.2.
Bob Method

A Plain Method that is not a Place Method.

Example:

Further explanation: The 'points' in the blue line of this Plain Method, such as those circled in green, show that a Place is not Made between changes in the direction of Hunting. This Method is therefore a Bob Method. (This Method is Double Oxford Bob Minor.)

2.
Treble Dodging Method

A Hunter in which:
1. The Path of the Hunt Bell is the same if it is rung backwards;
2. The Hunt Bell rings more than twice in each Place of the Path during a Plain Lead;
3. The Hunt Bell rings the same number of times in each Place of the Path during a Plain Lead;
4. The Hunt Bell Makes a Place exactly twice within a Plain Lead; and
5. The Hunt Bell is not a Stationary Bell.

Example:

Further explanation: In this Method, the treble (which is the only hunt bell) rings four times in each Place in a Plain Lead. The treble also has the same Path if it is rung forwards or backwards. The treble Makes a Place twice during a Plain Lead, once in 6th's and once in 1st's. This Method is therefore a Treble Dodging Method. (This Method is Kent Treble Bob Minor.)
'Dodging' in this term refers to a step back in an otherwise Hunting Path. For example, if the Hunting Path 2, 3, 4, 5 is modified to become 2, 3, 4, 3, 4, 5, then the step back in 3-4 is referred to as a dodge.

2.1.
Treble Bob Method

A Treble Dodging Method in which no bell Makes an Internal Place at any Cross Section, or which does not have any Cross Sections.

Example:

Further explanation: At this Treble Dodging Method's Cross Sections, which are cicled in green, there are no Intenal Places Made. The Places Made at both Cross Sections are 1st's and 6th's, which are external Places. Since this Method has palindromic symmetry (the Place Notation for the second half of the lead is the reverse of the Place Notation for the first half of the lead) there is no need to check the Cross Sections in the second half of the lead as these will have the same Places Made as in the first half of the lead. However for asymmetric (i.e. non-palindromic) Methods, all Cross Sections must be considered when further classifying a Treble Dodging method. This Method is therefore a Treble Bob Method. (This Method is Oxford Treble Bob Minor.)

2.2.
Surprise Method

A Treble Dodging Method in which one or more bells Make an Internal Place at every Cross Section.

Example:

Further explanation: An Internal Places is Made at both of this Treble Dodging Method's Cross Sections, which are cicled in green. 4th's is Made at the 2-3 Cross Section, and 3rd's is Made at the 4-5 Cross Section. (See the note about palindromic symmetry in Section C.2.1 above, which applies here.) This Method is therefore a Surprise Method. (This Method is Cambridge Surprise Minor.)

2.3.
Delight Method

A Treble Dodging Method that is neither a Treble Bob Method nor a Surprise Method.

Example:

Further explanation: An Internal Place is Made at the 2-3 Cross Section of this Treble Dodging Method, but an Internal Place is not made at the 4-5 Cross Section (the Cross Sections are cicled in green). (See the note about palindromic symmetry in Section C.2.1 above, which applies here.) Since Internal Places are Made at some but not all Cross Section Changes, this Method is therefore a Delight Method. (This Method is College Bob IV Delight Minor.)

3.
Treble Place Method

A Hunter in which:
1. The Path of the Hunt Bell is the same if it is rung backwards;
2. The Hunt Bell rings the same number of times in each Place of the Path during a Plain Lead;
3. The Hunt Bell Makes a Place more than twice within a Plain Lead;
4. The Hunt Bell is not a Stationary Bell.

Example:

Further explanation: This Method is Stoke Albany Treble Place Minor. Other examples are: Primrose Treble Place Minor and Alderney Treble Place Minor.

4.
Alliance Method

A Hunter in which:
1. The Path of the Hunt Bell is the same if it is rung backwards;
2. The Hunt Bell does not ring the same number of times in each Place of the Path during a Plain Lead;
3. The Hunt Bell is not a Stationary Bell.

Example:

Further explanation: This Method is Alperton Alliance Minor. Since the treble, which is the only Hunt Bell, rings twice in 1st's, 2nd's, 3rd's and 4th's Places, and rings four times in 5th's and 6th's Places, it meets requirement 2 above, together with the other requirements for an Alliance Method.
Another example is: Semiquincentenary Alliance Major.

5.
Little Method

A Plain, Treble Dodging, Treble Place or Alliance Method in which:
1. The Path of the Hunt Bell does not include ringing in all Places of the Method's Stage.

Example:

Further explanation: This Method is Canterbury Little Bob Minor. Since the treble, which is the only Hunt Bell, only rings in the first four Places, whereas the Stage of the Method is six, this is a Little Method.

D.
Method Classes: Hunters with More than One Hunt Bell
1.

If a Hunter has more than one Hunt Bell, it has the classification of the first Method Class found, if any, when inspecting each of the Hunt Bells, using the order: Plain Method, Treble Dodging Method, Treble Place Method, Alliance Method.

2.
Little Method

If the Path(s) of the Hunt Bell(s) of the first Method Class found in D.1 above, if any, all meet the definition of a Little Method per Section C.5 above, then the Hunter is classified as a Little Method.

3.
Slow Course Method

If the first Method Class found in D.1 above, if any, is a Plain Method, the Method is further classified in accordance with Sections C.1.1 and C.1.2 above, unless
(1) the Method has a first Hunt Bell with the Path for a Plain Method,
(2) the Method has a second Hunt Bell with the Path for a Little Treble Place or Little Alliance Method, and
(3) the second Hunt Bell Makes 2nd's Place over the first Hunt Bell when the latter is leading,
in which case the Method has a classification of Slow Course.

4.

If the first Method Class found in D.1 above, if any, is a Treble Dodging Method, the Method is further classified in accordance with Section C.2.1, C.2.2 and C.2.3 above, using the following:
If the Method has more than one Treble Dodging Hunt Bell, none of which are Little, or all of which are Little, the Cross Section Changes are all the Changes that are a Cross Section Change for any Treble Dodging Hunt Bell.
If the Method has both Little Treble Dodging Hunt Bell(s) and non-Little Treble Dodging Hunt Bell(s), the Cross Section Changes are all the Changes that are a Cross Section Change for any non-Little Treble Dodging Hunt Bell.
