4. Method Classification

A.
Definitions
1.
Method Class

A group of Methods whose Plain Lead and/or Plain Course contain the same, defined structural features.

2.
Hunt Bell

A bell that ends a Plain Lead in the same Place as it started.

Example:

Further explanation: The place notation recorded in the Central Council’s Method Library for the Method Plain Bob Minimus is x14x14x14x12. This place notation produces one Plain Lead when applied once to an initial Row. Using an initial Row of 1234 (Rounds), the Plain Lead shown in the diagram is produced. To identify which are the Hunt Bell(s), look at the final Row produced (in this example it is 1342). The Hunt Bell(s) are the bell(s) that are in the same Place(s) in the final Row as they were in the initial Row. In Plain Bob Minimus, the treble is therefore the only Hunt Bell.

3.
Working Bell

A bell that ends a Plain Lead in a different Place from where it started.

Further explanation: Using the example in A.2 above, 2, 3 and 4 are Working Bells because at the final Row of a Plain Lead they have not returned to the same Places they were in at the initial Row.

4.
Path

The sequence of Places that a Method causes a given bell to occupy.

Further explanation: A Path is often represented by drawing a line through a bell as it progresses through the Rows of a Block. The treble’s Path is shown this way using a red line in the example in A.2 above.

5.
Plain Hunting

A Path that is formed by progressing from an earlier Place to a later Place, or vice versa (but not a combination of both), at the rate of one Place per Row. (Alternative form: a bell Plain Hunts.)

Further explanation: In the diagram in A.2 above, the treble Plain Hunts up in the first half of the lead, and then Plain Hunts down in the second half of the lead.

6.
Place-Making

Occupying the same Place for two or more consecutive Rows.

Further explanation: See the example of Place-Making in C.1.1 below.

7.
Making a Place

Occupying the same Place for exactly two consecutive Rows. (Alternative form: a bell Makes a Place.)

Further explanation: In the example in A.2 above, the treble Makes 4th's at the half lead, and Makes 1st's at the lead end.

8.
Making an Internal Place

Making a Place that is neither the first Place nor the last Place of the Stage of the Method being executed. (Alternative form: a bell Makes an Internal Place.)

Further explanation: See the examples of Making an Internal Place in C.2.2 and C.2.3 below.

9.
Dodging Places

Successive pairs of adjacent Places within a Row. Places 1 and 2 form the first pair of dodging Places, 3 and 4 the second, and so on. If the Row contains an odd number of Places, the last Place is considered to be its own Dodging Place.

Example: In Minor, the Dodging Places are 1-2, 3-4 and 5-6.

10.
Cross Section

A Change at which the Hunt Bell in question crosses from one pair of Dodging Places to the next.

Example: In Minor, the Cross Sections are the Changes that cause the Hunt Bell to (1) move from 2nd's Place to 3rd’s Place, and (2) move from 4th’s Place to 5th’s Place. See the examples in C.2.1, C.2.2 and C.2.3 below.

B.
Method Types
1.
Principle

A Static Method that does not use Jump Changes, that has no Hunt Bells, and in which all the Working Bells first return to their starting Places after the same number of Plain Leads.

Example:

Further explanation: A Plain Lead of the Method 34x34.14.12.14, starting from Rounds, ends with the Row 2341, as shown in the diagram. At the end of one Plain Lead, no bell has returned to the Place it was in at the initial Row (1234), so this method has no Hunt Bells. Continuing to apply the place notation successively produces the 3 additional leads shown, such that after 4 Plain Leads, Rounds is obtained. This is the Method’s Plain Course. Since (1) this Method has no Hunt Bells and (2) all Working Bells (which in this case is all bells because there are no Hunt Bells) first return to their starting Places after the same number of Plain Leads (in this case 4 Plain Leads), it is a Principle. (This Method is Stanton Minimus.)

2.
Hunter

A Static Method that does not use Jump Changes, that has one or more Hunt Bells, and in which either (a) all the Working Bells first return to their starting Places after the same number of Plain Leads, or (b) there are no Working Bells.

Example:

Further explanation: A Plain Lead of the Method x14x14x14x12, starting from Rounds, ends with the Row 1342, as shown in the diagram. At the end of one Plain Lead, the treble is the only bell that has returned to the Place it was in at the initial Row (1234), so the treble is this Method's only Hunt Bell. Continuing to apply the place notation successively produces the 2 additional leads shown, such that after 3 Plain Leads, Rounds is obtained. This is the Method’s Plain Course. Since (1) this Method has at least one Hunt Bell and (2) all Working Bells (which in this case are bells 2, 3 and 4) first return to their starting Places after the same number of Plain Leads (in this case 3 Plain Leads), it is a Hunter. (This Method is Plain Bob Minimus.)

3.
Differential Principle

A Static Method that does not use Jump Changes, that has no Hunt Bells, and in which the Working Bells do not all first return to their starting Places after the same number of Plain Leads.

Example:

Further explanation: A Plain Lead of the Method 5.1.5.1.5.1.5.1.5.345, starting from rounds, ends with the Row 31254, as shown in the diagram. At the end of one Plain Lead, no bell has returned to the Place it was in at the initial Row (12345), so this Method has no Hunt Bells. Continuing to apply the place notation successively produces the 5 additional leads shown, such that after 6 Plain Leads, Rounds is obtained. This is the Method’s Plain Course. It will be seen that bells 4 and 5 return to their starting Places at the end of the 2nd, 4th and 6th leads, whereas bells 1, 2 and 3 return to their starting Places at the end of the 3rd and 6th leads. Because the Working Bells do not all first return to their starting Places after the same number of Plain Leads, this Method is a Differential. Because it also has no Hunt Bells, it is a Differential Principle. (This method is Christ Church Dublin Differential Doubles.)

4.
Differential Hunter

A Static Method that does not use Jump Changes, that has one or more Hunt Bells, and in which the Working Bells do not all first return to their starting Places after the same number of Plain Leads.

Example:

Further explanation: A Plain Lead of the Method x16x16x36x16x16x1234, starting from rounds, ends with the Row 132645, as shown in the diagram. At the end of one Plain Lead, the treble is the only bell that has returned to the Place it was in at the initial Row (123456), so the treble is this Method's only Hunt Bell. Continuing to apply the place notation successively produces the 5 additional leads shown, such that after 6 Plain Leads, Rounds is obtained. This is the Method’s Plain Course. It will be seen that bells 2 and 3 return to their starting Places at the end of the 2nd, 4th and 6th leads, whereas bells 4, 5 and 6 return to their starting Places at the end of the 3rd and 6th leads. Because the Working Bells do not all first return to their starting Places after the same number of Plain Leads, this Method is a Differential. Because it also has at least one Hunt Bell, it is a Differential Hunter. (This method is Deferential Differential Bob Minor.)

C.
Hunters and Differential Hunters with One Hunt Bell
1.
Plain Method

A Hunter or Differential Hunter in which:
1. The Path of the Hunt Bell is the same if it is rung backwards;
2. The Path of the Hunt Bell includes ringing in 1st's Place; and
3. The Hunt Bell rings exactly twice in each Place of the Path during a Plain Lead.

1.1.
Place Method

A Plain Method in which the Paths of all the bells consist only of Plain Hunting and Place-Making, and in which a change in the direction of Plain Hunting is separated by Place-Making.

1.2.
Bob Method

A Plain Method that is not a Place Method.

2.
Treble Dodging Method

A Hunter or Differential Hunter in which:
1. The Path of the Hunt Bell is the same if it is rung backwards;
2. The Path of the Hunt Bell includes ringing in 1st's Place;
3. The Hunt Bell rings exactly four times in each Place of the Path during a Plain Lead; and
4. The Hunt Bell Makes a Place exactly twice within a Plain Lead.

2.1.
Treble Bob Method

A Treble Dodging Method in which no bell Makes an Internal Place at any Cross Section, or which does not have any Cross Sections.

2.2.
Surprise Method

A Treble Dodging Method in which one or more bells Make an Internal Place at every Cross Section.

2.3.
Delight Method

A Treble Dodging Method that is neither a Treble Bob Method nor a Surprise Method.

3.
Alliance Method

A Hunter or Differential Hunter in which:
1. The Path of the Hunt Bell is the same if it is rung backwards;
2. The Path of the Hunt Bell includes ringing in 1st's Place;
3. The Hunt Bell rings either twice or four times in each Place of the Path during a Plain Lead; and
4. The Hunt Bell Makes a Place exactly twice within a Plain Lead.

4.
Treble Place Method

A Hunter or Differential Hunter in which:
1. The Path of the Hunt Bell is the same if it is rung backwards;
2. The Path of the Hunt Bell includes ringing in 1st's Place;
3. The Hunt Bell rings the same number of times in each Place of the Path during a Plain Lead;
4. The Hunt Bell Makes a Place more than twice within a Plain Lead; and
5. The Path of the Hunt Bell has exactly two points of Symmetry.

5.
Little Method

A Plain, Treble Dodging, Alliance or Treble Place Method in which:
1. The Path of the Hunt Bell is restricted to fewer Places than the Stage of the Method.

D.
Hunters and Differential Hunters with More than One Hunt Bell
1.

If a Hunter or Differential Hunter has more than one Hunt Bell, it has the classification of the first Method Class found, when inspecting each of the hunt bells, using the order: Plain Method, Treble Dodging Method, Alliance Method, Treble Place Method.

If the Path(s) of the Hunt Bell(s) of the first Method Class found above are all restricted to fewer Places than the Stage of the Method, then the Hunter or Differential Hunter is also classified as a Little Method.

When the first Method Class found above is a Plain Method, the Method is further classified in accordance with Sections C.1.1 and C.1.2 above.

When the first Method Class found above is a Treble Dodging Method, the Method is further classified in accordance with Section C.2.1, C.2.2 and C.2.3 above. If the Method has more than one Treble Dodging Hunt Bell, the Cross Section Changes are all the Changes in which any Treble Dodging Hunt Bell crosses from one pair of Dodging Places to the next.

E.
Methods that are Currently Unclassified
1.

Dynamic Methods, and Static Methods that use Jump Changes, are not currently incorporated in the classification system as defined in Sections B, C and D above. Furthermore, Methods may have a Type of Hunter or Differential Hunter, but not classify any further under Sections C and D above. For the avoidance of doubt, this does not restrict any of these Methods from being used to produce Touches under Section 3.I.

F.
Construction of Class Name
1.
Class Name

Class Name is constructed by concatenating up to three terms as follows:
1. Add the term "Differential" for a Differential Principle or a Differential Hunter;
2. Add the term "Little" for a Little Method; and
3. Add one of the following classification terms if it applies: Place, Bob, Treble Bob, Surprise, Delight, Alliance, Treble Place.
If none of these terms apply, including when a Method is unclassified, the Method has no Class Name.
