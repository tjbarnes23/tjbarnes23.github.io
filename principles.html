2. Design Principles

A
Principles

The framework is being developed using the following principles:

(1)

Description not prescription (the "permissive" part of the CRAG proposal): Avoid arbitrary rules and value judgements. Seek to find the logical boundaries that define the limits of method ringing, and ensure the framework supports everything within these boundaries. The boundaries used are shown in section B below.

(2)

Simple, generic and consistent (the "simple" part of the CRAG proposal): The more straightforward the new framework is, the more widely it will be understood across the ringing community and the more accessible it will be to new ringers joining the Exercise. Standardise terms and requirements where this increases simplicity and doesn't lose any historical meaning. However, it is also recognised that method ringing has some inherent complexity resulting from both its mathematical foundation and its rich history, such that not all complexity can be eliminated.

(3)

Support all lengths of ringing: The CRAG mandate calls for a simple and permissive descriptive framework for ringing, not just peal ringing. Cover all lengths -- from short touches to record lengths.

(4)

Define and explain: Terms used in the framework should be clearly defined. Include examples and explanations to assist in the understanding of the framework.

(5)

Continuity: Most standard methods and performances should continue to be described as they are today, or with only minimal alterations in the interests of standardisation and simplification.

B
Boundaries

For the purposes of the new framework, the following boundaries are being used in defining the scope of method ringing:

(1)
Rows

In method ringing, the same set of bells rings in every row, with each bell ringing exactly once in every row. The minimum number of bells in a row is one.

(2)
Places

In method ringing, each bell rings in a different place in the row to every other bell.

(3)
Changes

A change may be any transformation of one row to the next. This results in the definition of jump changes and identity changes in the new framework. The term adjacent change has also been introduced to be equivalent to the previous definition of "change" in Decision (E) A.1.

(4)
Methods

In general, methods are sequences of changes that have been given names and which are recorded in the Central Council's method collections. Any sequence of changes should be able to be named as a method.

(5)
Cover Bells

A row may include one or more cover bells. Cover bells that remain in the same place in every row (such places are referred to in the framework as fixed cover places) are excluded when determining truth.

(6)
Truth

Truth is based upon the n/n+1 principle, i.e. 0 to n complete extents + 1 optional partial extent. In other words a block of rows is true if either (a) all of its rows are distinct (only occur once) or (b) when the block is longer than an extent, any rows that remain after removing all complete extents are distinct. The location of individual rows within a block of rows does not have any bearing on truth.

C
Additional Considerations

Following are some additional considerations for the framework's design:

(1)
Length and Stage

Ringing of all lengths on all stages should be treated alike for simplicity, consistency and permissiveness. The definitions should be relevant and applicable to all ringing, not just peals. For example, the definition of true applies equally to short touches, quarter peals and peals, and if 5000 changes is a peal of Major, then it is also sufficient for a peal of Minor.

(2)
Performance reporting

The Conditions for Peal Ringing in Decision (D) are being replaced by performance reporting requirements. Under this new permissive framework, the onus is placed upon the bands ringing a performance to determine if a performance merits publication. The requirement is to report any non-standard characteristic in the performance report such that this can be used in subsequent analyses.

(3)
Spliced

The term "spliced" should be standardised for simplicity and consistency to mean any composition or performance that involves more than one method.
